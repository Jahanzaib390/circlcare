CirclCare AI

CirclCare AI is a multilingual, agent-assisted mobile platform for coordinating informal elder-care services across families, caregivers, drivers, nurses, physiotherapists, pharmacy runners, clinic helpers, home attendants, and trusted local providers.

Expo React Native app Node.js and Express backend OpenAI-compatible LLM abstraction Mock data for Lahore, Karachi, Islamabad Agentic matching, pricing, and dispute flows

1. Product Overview

Families often coordinate elder-care work manually through WhatsApp, phone calls, references, and repeated follow-up. A single request can involve multiple real-world constraints: a wheelchair-friendly clinic ride, a trusted companion, a female nurse, a physiotherapist who speaks the patient's language, a pharmacy stop after consultation, or a recurring caregiver.

CirclCare AI turns noisy natural-language requests into structured service plans, then matches the family with trusted providers using safety, language, availability, reliability, distance, pricing, and family preference signals.

One-line Pitch

CirclCare AI is a multilingual life-admin agent for elder-care services that matches families with trusted local providers using safety, preferences, language, availability, reliability, and transparent pricing.

Primary Differentiator

The product is not a generic home-services marketplace. It focuses on high-trust elder-care workflows where the best match is often not simply the nearest provider.

Demo Coverage

The seeded data and demo scenarios cover clinic visits, recurring caregivers, home physiotherapy, food and medicine support, fallback flows, noisy Roman Urdu, and code-switched requests.

Supported Service Categories

  • Health and care: home nurse, physiotherapy, caregiver, elder companion, lab sample collection.
  • Mobility and appointments: clinic visit, wheelchair support, hospital appointment companion, medicine stop.
  • Errands: medicine pickup, prescription refill, lab report pickup, document carrying.
  • Food and daily support: diabetic meal provider, low-salt food, daily support, meal plans.
  • Recurring care: daily caregiver, weekly physiotherapy, monthly medicine refill.

Main App Flow

  1. User enters or speaks a natural-language elder-care request.
  2. The backend parses intent, services, patient, location, time, mobility needs, risk, and preferences.
  3. The app shows a confirmation screen for family review.
  4. The backend filters and scores providers, then an agentic tool loop reviews candidate evidence.
  5. The app displays ranked matches with human-readable reasons and transparent score factors.
  6. The pricing engine returns an itemized quote and optional cheaper-slot suggestion.
  7. The booking simulator advances lifecycle status for demo purposes.
  8. The dispute flow reviews evidence and recommends refund, revisit, escalation, or no action.

4. Matching Factors

CirclCare uses a two-stage matching model. Stage 1 applies hard filters that can reject unsafe or incompatible providers. Stage 2 scores the remaining providers using weighted factors. The agentic step then reviews observed evidence and can adapt the final ranking beyond a simple distance-first baseline.

Stage 1: Hard Filters

Filter Behavior
Service typeProvider must offer at least one requested service.
Area coverageProvider must serve the area by name match or be inside service radius using distance calculation.
AvailabilityProvider must have an availability slot compatible with the requested time.
Booking conflictProvider is rejected if already booked for an overlapping slot. A next-slot suggestion may be returned.
Home visitRequired for services that happen at the elder's home.
Gender requirementExplicit male/female required preferences are hard filters.
VerificationRequired for high-risk care, verified-only preference, home nursing, physiotherapy, and lab sample requests.
Wheelchair supportRequired when the request includes wheelchair needs.
Language requirementIf language is explicitly required, provider must speak at least one requested language.

Stage 2: Weighted Scoring

Factor Default Weight How It Is Calculated
Specialization0.20Compares provider specialties against service bundle, patient context, and mobility needs.
Availability fit0.15Uses number of available slots and daily capacity.
Reliability0.15Uses provider on_time_score.
Language0.10Measures overlap between requested languages and provider languages.
Gender comfort0.10Rewards preferred gender where applicable after hard requirements are handled.
Rating recency0.10Combines recent review score and lifetime rating, with more weight on recent reviews.
Cancellation risk0.10Inverts cancellation rate so lower cancellation improves score.
Distance0.05Scores closeness relative to provider service radius.
Price fit0.05Normalizes base rate and allows emergency requests to tolerate a premium.

Category-Specific Weight Adjustments

Certain service categories override default weights. For example, physiotherapy increases specialization and rating-recency weight, while clinic visits increase availability, distance, and reliability. Caregiver and home nurse flows increase reliability, gender comfort, specialization, language, and cancellation-risk importance.

Agentic Matching Step

After hard filtering and scoring, POST /api/match sends candidate summaries, rejected-provider reasons, conflict information, and the distance-only baseline provider into an LLM tool loop. The returned response includes:

  • agent_trace: tools called and observations inspected.
  • agent_decision: selected provider IDs and reasoning.
  • baseline_comparison: whether the agent agreed with or adapted beyond the baseline.
Why this matters: The matching flow can explain both why a provider was selected and why other providers were rejected. This is important for trust, safety, and debugging.

5. Antigravity Workflow

The project was built with Antigravity as the primary development workflow. The implementation was not created as one large prompt. It was divided into phased conversations so each part of the product could be planned, implemented, reviewed, and continued with context.

How Antigravity Was Used

  1. An overall phase-wise action plan was created first. This became the build roadmap for the app, backend, data, agent behavior, demo flow, and submission artifacts.
  2. Each follow-up conversation focused on a specific phase or feature area, such as project setup, request capture, provider data, matching, pricing, booking simulation, dispute handling, fallback behavior, demo scenarios, polish, testing, and Android build configuration.
  3. For each phase, Antigravity was used to produce or refine implementation plans, edit files, run local checks, and continue from the previous work.
  4. Conversation artifacts were kept separately so the development process can be reviewed independently from the application code.
  5. The final project includes both the implemented source code and the separate conversation logs that show the staged AI-assisted build process.

Conversation Log Location

The Antigravity conversation exports are stored separately in Antigravity Logs/. Each numbered folder represents a separate conversation or work session. These logs are intended to be submitted separately from this standalone documentation file.

Cost Per User Journey

A normal full journey uses request parsing, provider matching, explanations for top candidates, quote review, and optionally a dispute flow. Expected LLM calls:

  • Low: 3 calls if explanations are cached or only the top match is explained.
  • Normal demo: 5-6 calls.
  • Heavy: 8+ calls if the family repeatedly clarifies and re-runs matching.

The prototype is designed to use a small routing model for most language tasks. Using GPT-5.4 mini as the planning baseline, the public OpenAI API pricing page lists standard text pricing at $0.75 / 1M input tokens, $0.075 / 1M cached input tokens, and $4.50 / 1M output tokens. Subscription and Unit Economics

The platform-level monthly numbers grow with usage, but the important sustainability metric is cost per family. A normal completed care request is estimated at about $0.0154 in LLM usage. If a family uses the app one or two times per month, the LLM cost for that family is about $0.02-$0.03/month.

Family Usage Pattern Estimated LLM Cost Example Revenue Model Sustainability Read
1 request/month About $0.015/month Small booking fee or provider commission LLM cost is almost negligible compared with service revenue.
2 requests/month About $0.03/month Low family subscription plus optional booking fee Even a very low monthly plan covers LLM usage many times over.
8 requests/month About $0.12/month Recurring-care family plan Higher usage improves retention while LLM cost remains low.

A pure subscription model may be difficult for families who only need help once or twice a month. A stronger model is hybrid: free request intake, a small confirmed-booking fee, provider commission on completed services, and an optional family care plan for recurring users. This aligns cost with actual service value instead of asking every family to pay heavily before they need care.

Cost interpretation: These are LLM-token estimates only. They do not include hosting, database, SMS/WhatsApp, maps, payment processing, observability, or human support costs. Caching, shorter prompts, Batch API for offline jobs, and fewer explanation calls can reduce spend.

Cost Controls

  • Use deterministic filters before any LLM call.
  • Send compact candidate summaries instead of full provider records.
  • Cache stable match explanations by request/provider pair.
  • Cap tool-loop steps.
  • Use cheaper models for parsing and reserve stronger models for high-risk decisions.
  • Keep dispute demo tools local and deterministic.

8. Baseline Comparison

The non-agentic baseline is simple: choose the first eligible provider by distance after hard filters. The agentic flow starts from the same eligible provider set but inspects additional evidence before deciding whether to agree with the baseline or adapt beyond it.

Baseline Rule

baseline = first eligible provider sorted by distance_km

Agentic Rule

agentic_match = hard filters + weighted scoring + tool observations + LLM decision

tool observations include:
- candidate providers
- rejected providers and rejection reasons
- calendar conflicts
- baseline provider
- reliability, cancellation, verification, specialization, language, and risk signals

Comparison Summary

The generated comparison file contains 18 scenarios. In some scenarios the agent confirms the distance baseline after checking evidence. In other scenarios it adapts because the distance-first provider is weaker on reliability, specialization, verification, family constraints, language, or cancellation risk.

Scenario Type Baseline Weakness Agentic Advantage
Recurring caregiverDistance-only does not optimize risk, specialization, or family constraints.Selects an agency with better verification, coverage, reliability, and specialization.
Home physiotherapyNearest provider may not be strongest for post-surgery rehab.Prioritizes orthopedic rehab, verification, and lower cancellation risk.
Food and medicine supportNearest care agency may not be best for medicine logistics.Selects provider specializing in prescription pickup and cold-chain medicines.
Failure and fallbackNearest provider may carry higher cancellation risk.Selects safer provider with stronger reliability and lower cancellation rate.

9. Assumptions

  • The prototype uses mock provider, booking, service, and user data. It does not connect to a live care-provider marketplace.
  • Provider ratings, cancellation rates, disputes, and availability slots are seeded demo values.
  • Area matching uses named service areas and approximate coordinates. Production would use verified addresses and a routing API.
  • Payments, refunds, and dispatch notifications are simulated. No real money is moved.
  • OpenAI can be used when an API key is configured, but the app also supports deterministic mock mode for offline demos.
  • Urdu and Roman Urdu handling are demonstrated through parser behavior, localization files, and robustness traces, but broad dialect coverage would require more testing.
  • Provider identity, background checks, licenses, and insurance are represented by demo fields such as verified; real onboarding would require operational verification.
  • The app is designed around family coordination for elder care, not emergency medical diagnosis or urgent clinical triage.

10. Privacy Note

The prototype uses seeded mock data and should not contain real patient records, real provider records, payment credentials, or protected medical information. User-entered requests may describe health or family situations, so production handling would require stronger controls.

Prototype Privacy Behavior

  • Seeded JSON data is used for providers, users, bookings, services, and demo scenarios.
  • The map demo uses seeded provider coordinates and a public mobile Google Maps key. Current-location requests require explicit phone permission before matching proceeds.
  • No real payment processing is performed.
  • No real provider dispatch happens.
  • No production database of patient data is included.
  • Conversation logs are stored separately and should be reviewed before external sharing to ensure they do not include secrets or private information.

Production Privacy Requirements

  • Encrypt personal, booking, location, and dispute data at rest and in transit.
  • Use authentication, authorization, role-based access, and audit logs.
  • Minimize data sent to LLMs by stripping unnecessary identifiers and using compact summaries.
  • Store agent traces with access controls because they may include sensitive care context.
  • Define retention policies for booking, location, dispute, and conversation data.
  • Add consent flows for family members and providers.

11. Limitations

Important: CirclCare AI is a coordination prototype. It is not a medical diagnosis system, not an emergency response service, and not a replacement for licensed clinical judgment.

  • Provider data is mocked and not verified against real-world credentials.
  • Availability and booking conflicts are simulated from local JSON data.
  • Map display is live through Google Maps, but matching still uses seeded provider coordinates and service radius rather than traffic-aware route navigation.
  • LLM parsing can be wrong for ambiguous, noisy, or mixed-language requests; low confidence should trigger clarification.
  • The matching algorithm depends on seeded quality signals. Real deployments need continuous provider quality monitoring.
  • The current dispute flow uses mock evidence such as simulated GPS and booking terms.
  • The pricing model is a transparent prototype, not a market-tested pricing engine.
  • The app does not yet include production authentication, payment gateway integration, provider onboarding, insurance checks, or live notification delivery.
  • Fairness controls are policy-level and code-level in the prototype. Production would need monitoring for disparate impact and user appeals.

Safety and Fairness Boundaries

  • Explicit gender requirements are allowed when they represent family comfort or safety needs in home-care contexts.
  • Caste, sect, ethnic, and religion-based matching are intentionally excluded.
  • High-risk care requires verified providers in the matching logic.
  • Rejected providers are returned with reasons so decisions can be inspected.
